## 1. Executive Summary

This report presents a comprehensive analysis of Pakistan's cybersecurity regulatory landscape with specific focus on the banking sector. The study examines six major regulatory frameworks governing cybersecurity practices in Pakistan, including the Prevention of Electronic Crimes Act 2016, National Cyber Security Policy 2021, PTA regulations, nCERT guidelines, SBP directives, and FIA enforcement mechanisms.

The banking sector was selected due to its critical role in national economic infrastructure and the heightened cybersecurity requirements imposed by multiple regulatory bodies. Through systematic analysis, this report extracts 15 key regulatory obligations categorized across six domains: Access Control, Data Protection, Incident Reporting, Logging & Monitoring, Risk Management, and Business Continuity.

The Regulatory Obligation Matrix presented herein serves as a practical compliance tool for banking institutions operating in Pakistan, mapping specific obligations to their regulatory sources, responsible authorities, and implementation considerations.

## 3. Regulatory Framework Analysis

### 3.1 Prevention of Electronic Crimes Act (PECA) 2016

#### 3.1.1 Overview

The Prevention of Electronic Crimes Act 2016 is Pakistan's primary legislation governing cybercrimes and electronic data protection. Enacted on August 11, 2016, PECA establishes criminal offenses related to unauthorized access, data breaches, and electronic fraud (Government of Pakistan, 2016).

#### 3.1.2 Key Provisions Relevant to Banking

| Section | Provision | Banking Relevance |
|---------|-----------|-------------------|
| Section 3 | Unauthorized access to information systems | Protects banking systems from intrusion |
| Section 4 | Unauthorized copying of data | Safeguards customer financial data |
| Section 5 | Interference with information systems | Ensures banking service availability |
| Section 6 | Unauthorized access to critical infrastructure | Banks classified as critical infrastructure |
| Section 14 | Electronic fraud | Directly addresses banking fraud |
| Section 36 | Service provider obligations | Data retention requirements |

#### 3.1.3 Compliance Obligations for Banks

Under PECA 2016, banks must:
- Implement access control mechanisms to prevent unauthorized system access
- Maintain data integrity and prevent unauthorized copying or modification
- Preserve electronic records for potential investigation purposes
- Report cybercrime incidents to designated authorities
- Cooperate with FIA in cybercrime investigations

### 3.2 National Cyber Security Policy 2021

#### 3.2.1 Overview

The National Cyber Security Policy 2021 provides a strategic framework for securing Pakistan's cyberspace. The policy emphasizes a whole-of-nation approach, recognizing cybersecurity as essential for national security and economic prosperity (Ministry of Information Technology, 2021).

#### 3.2.2 Policy Pillars

1. **Governance Framework**: Establishment of national cybersecurity governance structures
2. **Legal Framework**: Strengthening of cybercrime legislation
3. **Capacity Building**: Development of cybersecurity workforce
4. **Critical Infrastructure Protection**: Safeguarding essential services including banking
5. **Incident Response**: National-level coordination for cyber incidents
6. **International Cooperation**: Engagement with global cybersecurity community

#### 3.2.3 Banking Sector Implications

The policy mandates:
- Designation of banking as Critical Information Infrastructure (CII)
- Mandatory cybersecurity audits for financial institutions
- Appointment of Chief Information Security Officers (CISOs)
- Participation in national threat intelligence sharing
- Compliance with sector-specific security standards

### 3.3 Pakistan Telecommunication Authority (PTA) Regulations

#### 3.3.1 Overview

The Pakistan Telecommunication Authority regulates telecommunications services and has issued several directives relevant to cybersecurity in the banking sector, particularly concerning mobile banking and electronic payment services (PTA, 2023).

#### 3.3.2 Key PTA Regulations

**PTA Cybersecurity Regulations 2022:**
- Mandatory security audits for telecom service providers
- Customer data protection requirements
- Incident reporting obligations
- Network security standards

**Mobile Banking Security Guidelines:**
- Two-factor authentication requirements
- Encryption standards for mobile transactions
- SIM swap fraud prevention measures
- Customer notification requirements

#### 3.3.3 Compliance Requirements

| Requirement | Description | Timeline |
|-------------|-------------|----------|
| Security Audit | Annual third-party security assessment | Yearly |
| Data Localization | Customer data stored within Pakistan | Ongoing |
| Incident Reporting | Report breaches within 24 hours | Per incident |
| Encryption | AES-256 for data at rest, TLS 1.2+ for transit | Mandatory |

### 3.4 National Computer Emergency Response Team (nCERT) Guidelines

#### 3.4.1 Overview

The National Computer Emergency Response Team (nCERT) operates under the Cabinet Division and serves as Pakistan's national coordination center for cybersecurity incidents. nCERT has issued several baselines and guidelines applicable to critical sectors (nCERT, 2023).

#### 3.4.2 nCERT Security Baselines

**Baseline Security Controls:**
1. Asset Management and Inventory
2. Vulnerability Management
3. Access Control and Identity Management
4. Security Monitoring and Logging
5. Incident Response Procedures
6. Business Continuity Planning
7. Security Awareness Training

#### 3.4.3 Incident Reporting Framework

nCERT mandates a tiered incident reporting structure:

| Severity Level | Description | Reporting Timeline |
|----------------|-------------|-------------------|
| Critical | National security impact | Immediate (within 1 hour) |
| High | Significant service disruption | Within 6 hours |
| Medium | Limited impact on operations | Within 24 hours |
| Low | Minor security events | Within 72 hours |

#### 3.4.4 Banking Sector Coordination

Banks are required to:
- Register with nCERT as a critical infrastructure operator
- Participate in threat intelligence sharing programs
- Report significant cyber incidents through designated channels
- Participate in national cyber exercises when organized

### 3.5 State Bank of Pakistan (SBP) Regulations for Banking Sector

#### 3.5.1 Overview

The State Bank of Pakistan (SBP) is the primary regulator for the banking sector and has issued comprehensive cybersecurity regulations through various circulars and frameworks (SBP, 2023).

#### 3.5.2 Key SBP Circulars and Frameworks

**BPRD Circular No. 04 of 2023 - Technology Governance and Risk Management Framework:**
- Board-level oversight of IT and cyber risks
- Establishment of IT Steering Committee
- Mandatory CISO appointment
- Regular risk assessments

**EPD Circular No. 01 of 2022 - Digital Banking Security:**
- Multi-factor authentication for all digital channels
- Real-time fraud detection systems
- Customer transaction limits and controls
- Secure API integration standards

**BPRD Circular No. 06 of 2021 - Information Security:**
- Comprehensive information security policy requirements
- Data classification and handling procedures
- Third-party risk management
- Security incident management

#### 3.5.3 SBP Compliance Requirements

| Domain | Requirement | Compliance Indicator |
|--------|-------------|---------------------|
| Governance | Board-approved IT Security Policy | Annual policy review |
| Access Control | Role-based access with MFA | 100% critical systems |
| Data Protection | Encryption of sensitive data | AES-256 standard |
| Monitoring | 24/7 SOC operations | Real-time monitoring |
| Incident Response | Documented IR procedures | Testing quarterly |
| Business Continuity | DR site with RTO < 4 hours | Annual DR drills |
| Audit | Annual security audit by PBA-certified auditor | Clean audit report |

### 3.6 Federal Investigation Agency (FIA) Cybercrime Enforcement

#### 3.6.1 Overview

The Federal Investigation Agency (FIA) is Pakistan's premier law enforcement agency for investigating cybercrimes under PECA 2016. The FIA Cybercrime Wing handles complaints and investigations related to electronic crimes (FIA, 2023).

#### 3.6.2 Role in Banking Cybersecurity

The FIA serves multiple functions:
- Investigation of banking fraud and cyber theft
- Coordination with banks on fraud prevention
- Prosecution of cybercriminals
- Public awareness campaigns

#### 3.6.3 Reporting and Cooperation Requirements

Banks are required to:
- Report significant fraud incidents to FIA Cybercrime Wing
- Preserve electronic evidence for investigation
- Cooperate with FIA during investigations
- Provide requested data under proper legal authority
- Implement FIA-recommended fraud prevention measures

## 4. Sector Selection: Banking Industry

### 4.1 Rationale for Selection

The banking sector was selected for this analysis based on the following considerations:

1. **Critical Infrastructure Status**: Banks are designated as Critical Information Infrastructure under the National Cyber Security Policy 2021

2. **Regulatory Density**: The banking sector faces the highest density of cybersecurity regulations from multiple authorities (SBP, PTA, nCERT, PECA)

3. **High-Value Target**: Financial institutions represent prime targets for cybercriminals due to direct monetary gains

4. **Digital Transformation**: Pakistani banks are rapidly digitizing services, expanding the attack surface

5. **Customer Data Sensitivity**: Banks handle vast amounts of sensitive personal and financial data

### 4.2 Banking Sector Overview in Pakistan

Pakistan's banking sector comprises:
- 5 Public sector commercial banks
- 20+ Private commercial banks
- 5 Foreign banks
- 11 Microfinance banks
- Multiple Digital Payment Service Providers

All these institutions must comply with the regulatory framework analyzed in this report.

## 7. Gap Analysis and Implementation Challenges

### 7.1 Common Compliance Gaps

Based on industry observations, Pakistani banks commonly face gaps in:

1. **SOC Maturity**: Many banks lack mature 24/7 SOC capabilities
2. **Incident Reporting Timeliness**: Delays in meeting nCERT reporting timelines
3. **Third-Party Risk Management**: Incomplete vendor security assessments
4. **Data Classification**: Inconsistent implementation of classification schemes
5. **Log Retention**: Infrastructure challenges for 5-year retention requirements

### 7.2 Implementation Challenges

| Challenge | Description | Mitigation Strategy |
|-----------|-------------|---------------------|
| Resource Constraints | Limited cybersecurity budget and skilled workforce | Prioritize controls based on risk; consider managed services |
| Regulatory Overlap | Multiple authorities with overlapping requirements | Unified compliance framework mapping all regulations |
| Legacy Systems | Older systems may not support modern security controls | Phased modernization; compensating controls |
| Vendor Ecosystem | Complex third-party relationships | Centralized vendor risk management program |
| Awareness Gap | Insufficient security awareness among staff | Regular training and awareness programs |

## 8. Recommendations

### 8.1 Strategic Recommendations

1. **Establish Unified GRC Framework**: Implement a Governance, Risk, and Compliance framework that maps all regulatory requirements to unified controls

2. **Invest in SOC Capabilities**: Either build internal SOC capabilities or engage SBP-approved managed security service providers

3. **Automate Compliance Monitoring**: Deploy automated tools to continuously monitor compliance status across all regulatory requirements

4. **Enhance Incident Response**: Develop and regularly test incident response procedures aligned with nCERT and SBP reporting requirements

5. **Strengthen Third-Party Risk Management**: Implement comprehensive vendor risk assessment and continuous monitoring programs

### 8.2 Tactical Recommendations

1. Conduct gap assessment against this Regulatory Obligation Matrix
2. Prioritize remediation based on regulatory criticality and risk exposure
3. Establish quarterly compliance review cycles
4. Engage with regulators proactively on interpretation and implementation guidance
5. Participate in industry forums and threat intelligence sharing initiatives

## 9. Conclusion

Pakistan's cybersecurity regulatory framework for the banking sector has matured significantly, with comprehensive requirements spanning access control, data protection, incident reporting, logging and monitoring, risk management, and business continuity. The multi-regulator environment (SBP, PTA, nCERT, FIA) creates a complex compliance landscape that requires banks to adopt integrated governance approaches.

This analysis has identified and categorized 24 key regulatory obligations applicable to Pakistani banks, presented in a Regulatory Obligation Matrix that can serve as a practical compliance tool. The successful implementation of these requirements demands significant investment in people, processes, and technology, but is essential for protecting Pakistan's financial infrastructure and maintaining public trust in digital banking services.

The regulatory framework continues to evolve, and banks must establish mechanisms for continuous monitoring of regulatory changes and adaptive compliance programs. The recommendations provided in this report offer a roadmap for achieving and maintaining regulatory compliance while building a robust cybersecurity posture.
